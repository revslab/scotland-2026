Option A

🏝️ Islay Island

The whisky pilgrimage
The Vibe

Fly to a remote island of 3,000 people in the Inner Hebrides. White sand beaches, crashing Atlantic waves, and some of the most legendary distilleries on earth. This is a pilgrimage, not a tour.

🥃 Distilleries
  • ⭐Bruichladdich — The main event. Book the Warehouse Experience or Black Art tasting for the full experience, not just the standard tour
  • 🌾Kilchoman — Scotland's most remote farm distillery. They grow their own barley right there. Incredible ethos
  • 🏰Lagavulin — Warehouse tasting with views of Dunyvaig Castle ruins on the cove
  • 🦭Bunnahabhain — Most remote distillery on the island. Seals lounging on the rocks below. Least peaty Islay malt
🏛️ History & Nature
  • ✝️Kildalton Cross — 8th century Celtic high cross, one of the finest surviving in Scotland
  • 🏔️Machir Bay & Saligo Bay — Wild Atlantic beaches with dramatic cliffs
  • 🦅Sea eagles, seals, and coastal wildlife everywhere
🚗 Getting Around

Hire a private driver/guide (~£250–350/day). Everyone can drink freely, and you get a local insider who knows the island. Everything is within 30 min of everything else.

The Bruichladdich factor: There is no mainland tasting room, no satellite experience. If you want the real Bruichladdich experience — tasting cask-strength stuff that never leaves the island, standing in Port Charlotte smelling the sea air — this is the only way.
🌤️ July: 14–17°C, long daylight (17+ hrs), coastal breeze cuts the midges
Tradeoffs
✅ PROS
  • •Bruichladdich. No substitute
  • •Island feel — truly away from it all
  • •Private driver = zero stress
  • •Concentrated — no long drives
  • •July = peak daylight, mild temps
⚠️ CONS
  • •Flights limited (Loganair, book early)
  • •Less scenic variety than Highlands
  • •No grand megaliths/cairns
  • •July midges exist (coastal wind helps)
  • •Jess may want non-whisky activities — options are more limited on a small island
Option B

🏔️ Highland Road Trip

Scenery, standing stones & sleeper distilleries
The Vibe

Rent a car and drive through the Scottish Highlands. Ancient standing stones older than the pyramids, jaw-dropping glens, and craft distilleries that most tourists never find. Something for everyone.

🥃 Distilleries (Sleeper Hits)
  • 🏆Benromach (Forres) — Tiny, traditional, handcrafted Speyside. The anti-corporate distillery
  • 🔮The Cairn (Grantown-on-Spey) — Ultra-modern, brand new. Sister to Benromach — future meets past
  • 🌊Deanston (near Stirling) — Beautiful converted cotton mill on a river. Self-powered by waterwheel
  • 🤫Edradour (Pitlochry) — Smallest traditional distillery in Scotland. Feels like discovering a secret
  • 🏔️Tomatin — Gorgeous Highland setting, under most tourists' radar
🏛️ Ancient Sites & Scenery
  • 🪨Kilmartin Glen — 350+ ancient monuments in one valley. Standing stones, cairns, 5,000-year-old rock carvings. Barely anyone goes here. This is the hidden gem of Scotland
  • ⭕Clava Cairns (near Inverness) — 4,000-year-old burial cairns and standing stones. Atmospheric and uncrowded. Inspired Outlander's Craigh na Dun
  • ⛰️Glencoe — One of Scotland's most dramatic valleys. Jaw-dropping drive
  • 🌿Cairngorms National Park — Wild Highland scenery, hiking, wildlife
🚗 Getting Around

Rental car from Glasgow. More flexibility to explore, stop anywhere, adjust plans on the fly. Note: driving on the left + single-track Highland roads is an adventure in itself.

For Jess: More variety beyond whisky — stunning hikes, charming villages, ancient history, highland cows, gorgeous scenery at every turn. Easier to mix interests for the whole group.
☀️ July: 15–20°C, longest days of the year, but peak tourist season on popular routes
Tradeoffs

  • •No flights to coordinate

  • •No Bruichladdich (Islay only, period)

☀️ July in Scotland — What to Expect

🌡️ Weather

14–20°C (57–68°F). The warmest month, but this is Scotland — pack layers and a waterproof. Rain is always possible. Islay gets more wind; mainland Highlands can be warmer inland.

🌅 Daylight

17–18 hours of daylight. Sun doesn't fully set until ~10pm, and it never gets truly dark. Incredible for exploring and photography.

🦟 Midges

July is peak midge season, especially in sheltered inland areas (Highlands). Islay's coastal wind helps significantly. Pack Smidge repellent either way — it's the Scottish-made secret weapon.

👥 Crowds

Peak tourist season. Popular mainland spots (Edinburgh, Glencoe, Skye) get busy. Islay stays relatively quiet — it's too remote for the tour bus crowd. Speyside is middle ground.

🥃 The Bruichladdich Question

There's no mainland consolation prize. No tasting room in Glasgow or Edinburgh. Their entire identity is Islay — the terroir, the water, the sea air. If Bruichladdich is a bucket list item, you have to go to the island. If great whisky in general is the goal, the mainland has phenomenal options you've probably never heard of.
